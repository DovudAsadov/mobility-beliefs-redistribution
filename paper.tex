\section{Introduction}

A central puzzle of political economy is that majorities of citizens whose income lies
below the mean rarely vote for the level of redistribution implied by the
\citet{meltzer1981rational} median-voter model. Empirical support for redistribution is
markedly weaker than the prevailing inequality would suggest \citep{hoy2021relatively,
alesina2018intergenerational}. \citet{benabou2001social} resolve this puzzle through the
``Prospect of Upward Mobility'' (POUM) hypothesis: when individuals expect their future
income to rise above the mean, they oppose redistribution today because it would tax their
own future selves. \citet{piketty1995social} formalises a complementary belief-formation
mechanism in which families update from their own mobility experiences, while
\citet{hirschman1973changing} describe a ``tunnel effect'' through which observing the
upward mobility of others raises tolerance for current inequality. Subsequent work shows
that \emph{fairness beliefs}---whether inequality is perceived to result from effort or
from injustice---also shape redistribution demand
\citep{alesina2005fairness, benabou2006belief, alesina2018intergenerational}.

\textit{Issue / problem.} Three gaps remain. First, the POUM mechanism has been tested
mostly on the United States \citep{alesina2005preferences} or on a single transition
country in the 1990s \citep{ravallion2000wants}; the only multi-country transition study
\citep{cojocaru2014prospects} pre-dates the post-2010 reform wave, uses a binary
EU/non-EU split, and conflates retrospective and prospective beliefs. Second, no
study has explicitly tested whether the POUM effect is \emph{moderated} by individuals'
attributions of inequality to effort versus injustice, despite clear theoretical
predictions \citep{alesina2005fairness, benabou2006belief}. Third, the prior literature
has not exploited the ten-step wealth ladder of LiTS, which yields a continuous expected
mobility measure that is comparable across very different income regimes.

A complementary strand uses information experiments to show that beliefs about one's own
and society's mobility prospects causally shape redistribution demand
\citep{kuziemko2015elastic, karadja2017richer, roth2018expected,
stantcheva2021perceptions}; whether the same mapping holds in post-socialist settings
remains open \citep{grosfeld2010comparing}.

\textit{Solution / contribution.} This paper provides the first systematic test of POUM on
the Life in Transition Survey IV (LiTS IV, 2022--23), the most recent EBRD/World Bank wave
covering 37 economies and 37{,}478 respondents \citep{ebrd2024lits}. The contribution is
fourfold: (i) I separate retrospective, prospective and ``ladder-implied'' mobility beliefs
and estimate their distinct associations with two redistribution measures; (ii) I
introduce a fairness-belief moderator built on the LiTS IV ``most important factor for
success'' question \citep{alesina2005fairness, alesina2018intergenerational}; (iii) I
provide a joint ``horse-race'' specification that disentangles the broad-optimism channel
of attitudinal items from the self-interest channel of ladder-based items; and (iv) I
exploit cross-country variation across 37 economies---including Central Asia and the
Caucasus, absent from comparable European studies
\citep{jaime2019social, alesina2018intergenerational}.

\section{Purpose Statement / Objectives}

The purpose of this study is to estimate whether and how beliefs about upward income
mobility shape support for income redistribution across the 37 economies covered by the
Life in Transition Survey IV, and to assess whether the strength of any POUM-type effect
depends on respondents' attributions of success to effort versus injustice. Building on
\citet{benabou2001social, alesina2005fairness, alesina2018intergenerational}, the study
pursues four specific objectives.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item Quantify the partial association between three distinct mobility-belief
    measures---retrospective, prospective and expected ladder mobility---and two
    redistribution outcomes (a Likert agreement that the rich--poor gap should be reduced,
    and a binary willingness to pay more taxes for poverty reduction).
    \item Test whether these associations survive the inclusion of country fixed effects,
    standard demographic controls, and household income, with standard errors clustered
    at the country level.
    \item Examine fairness-belief heterogeneity by interacting each mobility variable
    with two indicators: belief that ``effort and hard work'' is the main driver of
    success, and belief that political connections or breaking the law (i.e.\ injustice)
    is the main driver.
    \item Assess external validity by comparing patterns across EU and non-EU transition
    economies and across regional sub-samples.
\end{enumerate}

% =====================================================================
\section{Research Questions and Hypotheses}

\textbf{Research questions.}
\textit{RQ1.} Across the 37 LiTS IV economies, does a higher belief in upward mobility---past or expected---predict lower support for income redistribution, holding constant
demographics and country effects?\\
\textit{RQ2.} Does the relationship between mobility beliefs and redistribution depend on
whether respondents attribute success in their society to effort or to injustice?\\
\textit{RQ3.} Are the patterns observed for Likert-scale redistribution agreement
consistent with those for a costlier behavioural proxy (willingness to pay higher taxes
for inequality reduction)?

\textbf{Hypotheses.} Following \citet{benabou2001social, alesina2005preferences,
alesina2005fairness, alesina2018intergenerational}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=18pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item[$H_{1}$:] \emph{POUM main effect.} More optimistic mobility beliefs are
    associated with weaker support for redistribution: standardised coefficients are
    expected to be negative.
    \item[$H_{2a}$:] \emph{Effort-amplification.} The negative POUM effect is stronger
    among respondents who believe success is driven by effort and hard work.
    \item[$H_{2b}$:] \emph{Injustice-attenuation.} The negative POUM effect is weaker (or
    reverses) among respondents who attribute success to political connections or
    illegality, who view current inequality as fundamentally unfair.
    \item[$H_{3}$:] \emph{Behavioural consistency.} The signs and significance patterns
    are reproduced when the dependent variable is the binary willingness-to-pay outcome.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{Empirical strategy.} The study uses cross-sectional individual-level data and
adopts the standard regression approach in the redistribution-preferences literature
\citep{alesina2005preferences, cojocaru2014prospects, alesina2018intergenerational}. The
core specification is a country-fixed-effects ordinary-least-squares (OLS) model:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{R}_{ic} \;=\; \alpha + \beta\, \widetilde{M}_{ic} + \gamma'\,X_{ic} + \delta_{c} + \varepsilon_{ic}
\label{eq:base}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{R}_{ic}$ is the standardised redistribution preference of individual
$i$ in country $c$, $\widetilde{M}_{ic}$ is one of three standardised mobility-belief
variables, $X_{ic}$ is a vector of demographic controls, and $\delta_{c}$ is a country
fixed effect. Standard errors are clustered at the country level
\citep{cameron2015practitioner}, which is the appropriate level of aggregation when the
key right-hand-side variation is partly cross-country
\citep{abadie2023should}. Standardising both sides allows coefficients to be read in
within-country standard-deviation units, mirroring \citet{alesina2018intergenerational}.

\textbf{Fairness-belief moderation.} To test $H_{2}$, I add an interaction term:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{R}_{ic} \;=\; \alpha + \beta\, \widetilde{M}_{ic} + \theta\, F_{ic}
+ \pi\,(\widetilde{M}_{ic}\times F_{ic}) + \gamma'\,X_{ic} + \delta_{c} + \varepsilon_{ic}
\label{eq:int}
\end{equation}
where $F_{ic}\in\{0,1\}$ flags either belief in effort or belief in
injustice (constructed from LiTS IV question Q4.06). This interaction approach follows
\citet{alesina2005fairness, benabou2006belief} and \citet{alesina2018intergenerational}.

\textbf{Variables.} \emph{Outcomes} (LiTS IV codebook): (a) Q4.01f, agreement that ``the
gap between the rich and the poor should be reduced'' on a 1--5 Likert (primary, pro-redistribution coded); (b) Q4.05e, a binary willingness to pay more taxes for poverty
reduction (robustness). \emph{Mobility beliefs}: (i) Q4.01b retrospective (``I have done
better than my parents''); (ii) Q4.01e prospective (``children born now will have a
better life''); (iii) expected ladder mobility = Q2.37 $-$ Q2.35, the difference between
expected position four years ahead and current position on the 10-step wealth ladder
\citep{cantril1965pattern, deaton2008income}. A retrospective ladder change (Q2.35 $-$
Q2.36) is also computed. \emph{Fairness moderator}: a binary effort indicator
($Q4.06=1$) and a binary injustice indicator ($Q4.06\in\{3,4\}$). \emph{Controls}: age,
age$^2$, female dummy, tertiary education, marital status, urban residence, and log
monthly household income. Demographic information for the primary respondent is
drawn from the household roster using the random-respondent code, following the LiTS IV
technical report.

\textbf{Robustness layer.} The base specification is supplemented with six pre-specified
checks: (i) population-weighted WLS using the LiTS IV \texttt{weight\_pop} variable for
representativeness; (ii) ordered-logit estimation on the 1--5 Likert outcome
\citep{wooldridge2010econometric}; (iii) a \emph{joint} regression in which all four
mobility variables enter simultaneously, isolating the marginal contribution of each
channel; (iv) sub-sample heterogeneity by current ladder position (bottom-3, middle,
top-3 rungs), which directly tests the Bénabou--Ok prediction that POUM bites harder for
those with most ``room to rise''; (v) cohort splits at year-of-birth 1980, motivated by
\citet{alesina2007goodbye} and \citet{giuliano2014growing}; and (vi) five-region splits
(EU-CEE, Western Balkans, EaP+Caucasus, Central Asia, SEMED+Türkiye) replacing the
binary EU/non-EU contrast in \citet{cojocaru2014prospects}.

\textbf{Limitations of the design.} (1) The data are cross-sectional, so identification
relies on selection-on-observables; results should be interpreted as conditional
correlations \citep{benabou2006belief}. (2) Mobility expectations are themselves shaped
by past macroeconomic shocks, raising reverse-causality concerns
\citep{piketty1995social, giuliano2014growing}. (3) Fairness beliefs and redistribution
preferences may be jointly determined by political socialisation
\citep{alesina2007goodbye}. I therefore report multiple specifications, including
country fixed effects, robust outcomes, and sub-sample splits, and frame all conclusions
in terms of associations rather than causal effects.

\section{Data Collection Procedures}

The study uses secondary microdata from the \emph{Life in Transition Survey IV}
(LiTS IV), conducted jointly by the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development
(EBRD) and the World Bank between October 2022 and July 2023 \citep{ebrd2024lits}. The
survey covers 37 economies---33 EBRD countries of operation plus four comparators
(Czechia, Estonia, Germany, and Italy)---and contains 37{,}478 face-to-face interviews
in respondents' main language. Sampling proceeds in three stages: (i) probability-proportional-to-size selection of primary sampling units within strata defined by
region and urban/rural status; (ii) random-walk household selection within each PSU; and
(iii) Kish-grid selection of two adult respondents per household (a knowledgeable
respondent for socio-economic items and a randomly selected primary respondent for
attitudinal items, including the redistribution and mobility questions used here)
\citep{ebrd2024lits}. Population-scaled weights (\texttt{weight\_pop}) are provided.
The microdata, codebook, and questionnaire are publicly available on the EBRD LiTS data
portal. No new human-subject data are collected; the analysis is entirely secondary,
fully reproducible from the public dataset, and exempt from institutional ethics review.


% =====================================================================
\section{Validation of Findings}

Validation rests on five concrete strategies, each delivered in the empirical
appendix. \emph{(i) Multiple operationalisations:} six mobility constructs (three
attitudinal, three ladder-based) and two redistribution outcomes; concordant signs across
them strengthen inference. \emph{(ii) Population-weighted WLS} (Table~\ref{tab:weighted})
re-estimates the main specification using the LiTS IV \texttt{weight\_pop} weights to
recover population-representative coefficients. \emph{(iii) Ordered-logit re-estimation}
(Table~\ref{tab:ologit}) treats the 1--5 Likert outcome as ordinal rather than continuous
\citep{wooldridge2010econometric}. \emph{(iv) Joint horse-race regression}
(Table~\ref{tab:joint}) puts all four mobility variables on the right-hand side
simultaneously, separating the broad-optimism and self-interest channels.
\emph{(v) Triangulation with prior evidence:} signs and magnitudes are compared with
\citet{alesina2005preferences, ravallion2000wants, cojocaru2014prospects,
alesina2018intergenerational}; convergence across very different samples constitutes
external corroboration.


% =====================================================================
\section{Data Analysis}

After list-wise deletion on the regression covariates, the main analytical sample is
$N\!\approx\!28{,}000$ respondents across 37 countries. All continuous variables on the
right-hand and left-hand side are $z$-standardised within the full sample so that
coefficients in Equation~\eqref{eq:base} are interpretable as standard-deviation effects
\citep{alesina2018intergenerational}. The base specification is OLS with country fixed
effects and country-clustered standard errors. I report (a) one univariate model per
mobility variable; (b) interaction models for fairness-belief moderation; and
(c) a linear-probability robustness model on the binary willingness-to-pay outcome. All
analyses are implemented in Python~3.12 with \texttt{pandas}, \texttt{statsmodels}, and
\texttt{pyreadstat}; the code, derived data, and outputs are version-controlled and
reproducible from the public LiTS IV \texttt{.dta} file using the scripts in
\texttt{analysis/}. Listwise deletion is documented (and the differential availability of
income reduces $N$ from 35{,}766 to about 28{,}000); a Heckman-type extension is left for
future work.

\section{Preliminary Findings}

Estimates from Equation~\eqref{eq:base} reveal a more nuanced picture than a simple POUM
hypothesis predicts (Table~\ref{tab:main}). Attitudinal mobility items load
\emph{positively} on redistribution support ($\beta=+0.121$, $p<0.001$ for the
parents-comparison item; $\beta=+0.098$, $p<0.01$ for the prospective ``children will
have a better life'' item), apparently contradicting $H_{1}$. The POUM signal appears
only once mobility is measured on the Cantril ladder: current ladder position
($\beta=-0.085$, $p<0.001$) and realised four-year ladder gain ($\beta=-0.049$,
$p<0.001$) are negatively associated with redistribution demand, while expected ladder
mobility carries a negative but insignificant coefficient ($\beta=-0.015$, $p=0.24$).

The \emph{joint horse-race} regression in Table~\ref{tab:joint} resolves much of this
tension. With all four mobility variables on the right-hand side simultaneously, both
ladder-based measures sharpen and turn unambiguously POUM-consistent: current ladder
$\beta=-0.128$ ($p<0.001$) and \emph{expected} ladder mobility $\beta=-0.057$
($p<0.001$). The two attitudinal items remain positive
($\beta_{\text{parents}}=+0.126$, $\beta_{\text{kids}}=+0.083$, both $p<0.01$). The
contrast is the central empirical pivot of the paper: ladder-based mobility captures the
self-interested POUM channel posited by \citet{benabou2001social}, whereas attitudinal
mobility items load on a generalised pro-social optimism that is correlated with, but
analytically distinct from, expected income trajectories
\citep{alesina2018intergenerational, stantcheva2021perceptions}. Failing to disentangle
the two---as in much of the prior literature---masks the POUM effect.

The fairness-belief interactions (Table~\ref{tab:int}) sharpen the picture further. For
prospective mobility, the interaction with the \emph{injustice} indicator is negative and
significant ($\pi=-0.077$, $p<0.001$), while the interaction with the \emph{effort}
indicator is positive and significant ($\pi=+0.069$, $p<0.01$). Effort-believers
translate optimism into stronger pro-redistribution attitudes; injustice-believers do
not. For expected ladder mobility, the injustice interaction is marginally significant
($\pi=-0.028$, $p\approx0.10$), pointing in the same direction.

Heterogeneity tests confirm context-dependence. Across the five LiTS IV regions
(Table~\ref{tab:regions}, Figure~\ref{fig:region}), prospective-mobility loads most
strongly positive in Central Asia ($\beta=+0.456$, $p<0.001$), is essentially zero in
EU-CEE, and is positive but weaker in the Western Balkans, EaP+Caucasus, and
SEMED+Türkiye---a finer pattern than Cojocaru's binary EU/non-EU split. By age cohort
(Table~\ref{tab:cohort}), the negative ladder-mobility coefficient is significant only
for respondents born before 1980 ($\beta=-0.028$, $p<0.05$) and disappears for the
post-Soviet cohort, consistent with the long-shadow channel in
\citet{alesina2007goodbye}. The ordered-logit re-estimation (Table~\ref{tab:ologit})
preserves all signs and now lifts \emph{expected} ladder mobility to significance
($\beta=-0.039$, $p<0.01$), as does the population-weighted WLS specification
(Table~\ref{tab:weighted}).

In sum, LiTS IV provides clear support for a refined POUM mechanism: when mobility is
measured behaviourally (the Cantril ladder), self-interest dominates and pulls demand for
redistribution down; when it is measured attitudinally, the same items pick up a
correlated optimism that pulls demand up. Both effects are conditional on fairness
beliefs and on the institutional region in which the respondent lives.

\section*{Appendix C: Replication Code}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Appendix C: Replication Code}

The full pipeline used to produce all tables and figures is included with this thesis:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=18pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item \texttt{analysis/build\_dataset.py} reads the LiTS IV \texttt{.dta} file and
    constructs cleaned variables.
    \item \texttt{analysis/regressions.py} estimates Equations~\eqref{eq:base} and
    \eqref{eq:int}, and writes the tables and figures in \texttt{output/}.
    \item All packages are pinned in the project's \texttt{uv} virtual environment.
\end{itemize}
