\section{Introduction}

One of the most counterintuitive phenomena in political economy is that the majority of
citizens whose income is below mean show weaker support for redistribution than the
median-voter model of \citet{meltzer1981rational} predicts. Empirical evidence also supports
that preference toward redistribution is weaker than the inequality level would suggest
\citep{hoy2021relatively, alesina2018intergenerational}. \citet{benabou2001social}
offered an explanation with the ``Prospect of Upward Mobility'' (POUM) hypothesis, which
stated that when individuals have a belief that their income and wealth would change in
the future, they do not support redistribution as it may impose higher taxes on them
later. Complementary to that, \citet{piketty1995social} found that families change their
belief based on their own mobility experiences, whereas \citet{hirschman1973changing}
found a ``tunnel effect'' in which observing the upward mobility of others increases
tolerance for current inequality. Subsequent work shows that fairness beliefs also shape
redistribution demand \citep{alesina2005fairness, benabou2006belief,
alesina2018intergenerational}.

Based on the literature review there are three important limitations. First, the POUM
mechanism has been tested mostly on the United States \citep{alesina2005preferences} or
on a single transition country in the 1990s \citep{ravallion2000wants}. Although
performed at multi-country level by \citet{cojocaru2014prospects}, it is conducted prior
to the wave of reforms that began in 2010, assumes a dichotomy between the EU and
non-EU, and mixes up retrospective and prospective views. Second, the existence of a
POUM effect has not yet been tested in conjunction with individuals' belief that their
effort is not rewarded, which is clearly predicted by theory \citep{alesina2005fairness,
benabou2006belief}. Third, the prior literature has not tried the ten-step wealth
ladder, which provides a continuous expected mobility measure that compares the mobility
of individuals with very different incomes.

Moreover, complementary experiments used true information about people's inequality and
social mobility to show that beliefs causally shape redistribution demand
\citep{kuziemko2015elastic, karadja2017richer, roth2018expected, stantcheva2021perceptions}.
Whether the same mapping holds in post-socialist settings remains unclear
\citep{grosfeld2010comparing}.

This paper presents the first systematic evaluation of POUM on the LiTS IV (2022--23).
My four contributions are as follows: (i) I separate out retrospective, prospective and
``ladder-implied'' mobility beliefs and estimate their differential association to two
measures of attitudinal redistribution, (ii) I introduce the fairness-belief moderator,
based on the survey question ``What is the most important factor for success'', which
was not used in previous similar European studies \citep{alesina2005fairness,
alesina2018intergenerational}, (iii) I provide a joint specification that separates the
generalised-optimism channel of attitudinal items from the self-interest channel of
ladder-based items, and (iv) I take advantage of the cross-country variation across the
37 economies, including Central Asia and the Caucasus, which has been absent in previous
similar European studies \citep{jaime2019social}.

\section{Objectives}

My study measures whether and how mobility expectations shape demand for progressive
redistribution across the 37 economies surveyed by the Life in Transition Survey IV
(LiTS IV). I decompose these effects into a self-interested channel operating through
respondents' current ladder position and mobility expectations \citep{benabou2001social}
and a generalised-optimism channel operating through attitudinal items in the spirit
of \citet{hirschman1973changing, benabou2006belief, alesina2018intergenerational},
with fairness beliefs moderating both \citep{alesina2005fairness}. I pursue three
subgoals. First, I regress the two redistribution outcomes (Likert agreement that
``the gap should be reduced'' and a binary willingness to pay more taxes) on the six
mobility measures (parents-comparison, four-year retrospective, prospective children,
current ladder, and expected and realised four-year ladder mobility) via OLS with
country fixed effects and country-clustered standard errors. Second, I distinguish the
self-interested and generalised-optimism channels through horse-race specifications
with the representative mobility variables included jointly, following
\citet{alesina2018intergenerational, stantcheva2021perceptions}. Third, I test
fairness-belief moderation by adding two interaction terms per mobility measure: a
dummy for belief that effort drives success and a dummy for belief that political
connections or breaking the law (injustice) drives success. Robustness via splits by
region, age cohort, and ladder position is reported as illustrative rather than as a
separate objective.

\section{Research Questions and Hypotheses}

\textbf{Research questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=28pt,itemsep=2pt,label=\textit{RQ\arabic*.}]
    \item Across the 37 economies, does a higher belief in upward mobility (past,
    current, or expected) predict support for income redistribution, conditional on
    demographics and country fixed effects?
    \item Is the mobility--redistribution association attributable to a
    \emph{self-interest} channel (operating through ladder-based measures) or to a
    \emph{generalised-optimism} channel (operating through attitudinal items)?
    \item Does the relationship depend on respondents' attribution of success to
    effort or to injustice?
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Hypotheses.} Following \citet{benabou2001social, alesina2005preferences,
alesina2005fairness, alesina2018intergenerational, hirschman1973changing}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=18pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item[$H_{1}$:] \emph{Dual-channel mobility effect.} \emph{Ladder-based} mobility
    measures, capturing self-interested POUM, predict weaker redistribution demand
    ($\beta<0$). \emph{Attitudinal} mobility measures, capturing generalised optimism,
    predict stronger redistribution support ($\beta>0$).
    \item[$H_{2}$:] \emph{Asymmetric fairness moderation.} Belief that effort drives
    success steepens the channel-specific slope, while belief that injustice drives
    success attenuates or reverses it. Redistribution then becomes a fairness-correcting
    demand independent of own prospects.
    \item[$H_{3}$:] \emph{Behavioural consistency.} Sign and significance patterns are
    reproduced when the dependent variable is the binary willingness-to-pay outcome.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Data Collection Procedures}

The Life in Transition Survey IV (LiTS IV) dataset \citep{ebrd2024lits} is used as the
main dataset for this study. The survey was conducted by the EBRD and the World Bank in
2022--2023 and covered 37 countries with 37{,}478 respondents. The sampling process was
designed in three stages: (i) primary sampling units were selected based on probability
proportional to size within strata defined by region and urban or rural status, (ii)
within each primary sampling unit, random-walk households were selected, and (iii) two
adult respondents per household were selected using a Kish grid. One is a knowledgeable
informant for socio-economic questions, and one is a randomly selected respondent for
attitudinal questions, which include the redistribution and mobility measures used in
this study. Population-scaled weights \texttt{weight\_pop} are provided.
\emph{Variables} (\cref{tab:vars}). Primary outcome: Q4.01f, 1--5 Likert agreement that ``the gap between
rich and poor should be reduced'' (pro-redistribution coded). Secondary outcome: Q4.05e,
a binary willingness to pay more taxes for poverty reduction. Mobility beliefs are six
measures: Q4.01b (vs.\ parents), Q4.01c (four-year retrospective), Q4.01e (prospective
children), Q2.35 (current ladder), Q2.37$-$Q2.35 (expected four-year mobility), and
Q2.35$-$Q2.36 (realised four-year mobility) \citep{cantril1965pattern, deaton2008income}.
Fairness moderators are binary indicators built from Q4.06 (effort $=1$; injustice
$\in\{3,4\}$). Controls: age, age$^2$, female, tertiary, married, urban, and log
monthly household income.


% =====================================================================
\subsection{Validation of Findings}

Five strategies, delivered in the appendix, validate the main finding. First, I test six mobility beliefs (three attitudinal,
three ladder-based) and two redistribution outcomes, where coefficient signs pointing
in the same direction within each channel strengthen inference (\cref{tab:main}).
Second, because the sample over- or under-represents some groups, I use
population-weighted WLS (\cref{tab:weighted}) and re-estimate the main specification
using the \texttt{weight\_pop} weights to recover population-representative
coefficients. Third, ordered logit: I re-run treating the 1--5 outcome as ordered
rather than continuous, removing the assumption that the gaps between values are
equal (\cref{tab:ologit}). Fourth, horse-race regression: in one regression I put all
four representative mobility variables at the same time, showing that each variable
contributes independently rather than being redundant (\cref{tab:joint}). Fifth, I compare coefficient signs and magnitudes against past research \citep{alesina2005preferences, ravallion2000wants,
cojocaru2014prospects, alesina2018intergenerational}. Sub-sample heterogeneity by
ladder position, age cohort (pre-/post-1980), and five-region splits is reported as
exploratory \citep{alesina2007goodbye, giuliano2014growing}.

\subsection{Data Analysis}

The base specification is OLS with country fixed effects and country-clustered
standard errors \citep{cameron2015practitioner, abadie2023should}:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{R}_{ic} \;=\; \alpha + \beta\,\widetilde{M}_{ic} + \gamma'\,X_{ic}
+ \delta_{c} + \varepsilon_{ic},
\label{eq:base}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{R}_{ic}$ is standardised redistribution support, $\widetilde{M}_{ic}$
is one of the six standardised mobility-belief variables, $X_{ic}$ is the control
vector, and $\delta_{c}$ is a country fixed effect. Standardising both sides allows
coefficients to be read in within-country standard-deviation units
\citep{alesina2018intergenerational}. To test the dual-channel hypothesis $H_{1}$
(RQ2), \cref{eq:base} is estimated with $\widetilde{M}_{ic}$ replaced by a vector of
four representative mobility measures and $\beta$ by the corresponding coefficient
vector, isolating each channel's marginal contribution \citep{stantcheva2021perceptions}. To test
$H_{2}$, I add a fairness interaction:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{R}_{ic} \;=\; \alpha + \beta\,\widetilde{M}_{ic} + \theta\,F_{ic}
+ \pi\,(\widetilde{M}_{ic}{\times}F_{ic}) + \gamma'\,X_{ic} + \delta_{c}
+ \varepsilon_{ic}.
\label{eq:int}
\end{equation}
After listwise deletion the analytical sample is $N{\approx}28{,}000$ across 37
countries (income availability reduces $N$ from $35{,}766$). Analyses use Python~3.12
(\texttt{pandas}, \texttt{statsmodels}, \texttt{pyreadstat}) and are reproducible from
the public \texttt{.dta} file via the scripts in \texttt{analysis/}. Identification
rests on selection-on-observables, so estimates are interpreted as conditional
correlations rather than causal effects \citep{benabou2006belief}.

\section{Discussion}

OLS estimates (\cref{tab:main}, with the country pattern in \cref{fig:country})
support the dual-channel hypothesis $H_{1}$. The parents-comparison item
($\beta=+0.121$, $p<0.001$) and the ``kids will have a better life'' expectation item
($\beta=+0.098$, $p<0.01$) are both positively linked to support for redistribution, as
predicted by generalised optimism. On the self-interest (POUM) side, current ladder
position ($\beta=-0.085$, $p<0.001$) and realised ladder gain ($\beta=-0.049$,
$p<0.001$) are both negative, while expected ladder mobility is not significant
($\beta=-0.015$, $p=0.24$). The horse-race specification (\cref{tab:joint}) addresses
RQ2 and sharpens the channel separation. With all four mobility variables included
jointly, both ladder-based measures become stronger and align with POUM (current ladder
$\beta=-0.128$, $p<0.001$; expected mobility $\beta=-0.057$, $p<0.001$), while the two
attitudinal items remain positive ($\beta_{\text{parents}}=+0.126$,
$\beta_{\text{kids}}=+0.083$, both $p<0.01$). Both parts of $H_{1}$ are therefore
jointly supported. This is the central empirical finding: ladder-mobility relates to
the self-interested POUM channel of \citet{benabou2001social}, while attitudinal items
tap into a more general sense of optimism about society, distinct from predicted income
gains \citep{alesina2018intergenerational, stantcheva2021perceptions}. Omitting either
channel masks the POUM effect.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/fig_country_means.png}
\caption{Country-level means of redistribution support (Q4.01f) versus prospective
mobility (Q4.01e), LiTS~IV. Each dot is one of 37 economies, colour denotes the
five-region grouping, and dotted lines mark the sample means.}
\label{fig:country}
\end{figure}

In \cref{tab:int} and \cref{fig:pred}, the fairness interactions show the
opposite-direction signs predicted by $H_{2}$ (effort positive, injustice negative).
For prospective mobility, the effort interaction is positive ($\pi=+0.069$, $p<0.01$),
with effort-believers strengthening the positive slope. The injustice interaction is
negative ($\pi=-0.077$, $p<0.001$), weakening the slope and pushing it toward zero at
the margin. Expected ladder mobility's injustice interaction is consistent in
direction, although marginal ($\pi=-0.028$, $p\approx 0.10$). Parents-comparison
interactions are not significant ($p>0.4$), so $H_{2}$ is partial.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{output/fig_pred_interaction.png}
\caption{Predicted standardised redistribution support against prospective mobility
belief, by fairness belief. Country-fixed-effects OLS with country-clustered SEs, and
shaded bands are 95\% CIs. The slope is steeper for effort-believers and flatter for
those who do not endorse effort as the main driver of success (see \cref{tab:int}).}
\label{fig:pred}
\end{figure}

Regional heterogeneity (\cref{tab:regions}, \cref{fig:region}) shows that prospective
mobility loads most strongly in Central Asia ($\beta=+0.456$, $p<0.001$). EaP+Caucasus
is significantly positive (+0.153, $p=0.01$), the Western Balkans marginally so (+0.133,
$p=0.07$), and SEMED+T\"urkiye essentially null (+0.022, $p=0.55$). EU-CEE is
essentially zero. By cohort (\cref{tab:cohort}), the negative ladder coefficient is
significant only for respondents born before 1980 ($\beta=-0.028$, $p<0.05$) and
disappears for younger respondents, consistent with the long-shadow effect in
\citet{alesina2007goodbye}. By current ladder position (\cref{tab:ladder}), the prospective-mobility coefficient rises from $\beta=+0.042$ at the bottom rungs to $\beta=+0.274$ at the top. The ordered-logit re-estimation (\cref{tab:ologit})
preserves all signs and makes expected ladder mobility significant
($\beta=-0.039$, $p<0.01$). $H_{3}$ receives partial confirmation (\cref{tab:wtp}). All
three mobility measures load positively on the willingness-to-pay outcome.
Attitudinal mobility matches the main-regression pattern, while expected ladder
mobility flips sign ($-0.015 \to +0.031$), so $H_{3}$ holds for the attitudinal channel
only, consistent with the dual-channel logic.

\label{fig:region}
\end{figure}

The findings support the dual-channel POUM mechanism of $H_{1}$: ladder mobility
(self-interest) suppresses redistribution preferences, attitudinal mobility pulls them
up, and both are shaped by fairness beliefs and regional context.

\section{Ethics}

Because no human participants are involved in this study, the ethical risks are
relatively low. However, some of the ethical issues need to be addressed. Firstly,
the study uses secondary analysis of the LiTS IV, which is a publicly available
dataset. The secondary analysis is anonymised, and this study is exempt from
institutional ethics review. The dataset is used strictly for academic purposes.

Another issue is to represent the results transparently and to interpret them
correctly. The study presents all findings transparently, including null results.
In addition, evidence that mobility beliefs reduce support for
redistribution could be misused for cutting social programmes. The study does not
support any policy. All data sources, software, and literature are cited, and the
analysis is designed to be fully reproducible from the publicly available dataset.
Replication code is openly available (Appendix~C).
